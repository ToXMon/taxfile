Subtopic 1: Economic Loss Doctrine and Software

I. Foundational Cases

A. Seely v. White Motor Co. — Origin of the Doctrine

Seely v. White Motor Co., 63 Cal.2d 9, 403 P.2d 145, 45 Cal.Rptr. 17 (1965)

Chief Justice Roger Traynor authored the opinion establishing what became the economic loss doctrine. The plaintiff purchased a truck under conditional sales contract; the truck failed to perform as warranted, causing economic losses (lost profits, repair costs). The plaintiff sued in both express warranty and strict tort liability.

Settled Law — Holding

The court affirmed liability on express warranty but rejected tort recovery for purely economic loss:

“Even in actions for negligence, a manufacturer’s liability is limited to damages for physical injuries and there is no recovery for economic loss alone.” 63 Cal.2d at 17, 403 P.2d at 151
“The distinction that the law has drawn between tort recovery for physical injuries and warranty recovery for economic loss is not arbitrary and does not rest on the ‘luck’ of one plaintiff in having an accident causing physical injury. The distinction rests, rather, on an understanding of the nature of the responsibility a manufacturer must undertake in distributing his products.” 63 Cal.2d at 17, 403 P.2d at 151

Reasoning pillars:

  1. Safety vs. performance: Tort law addresses unreasonable risks of harm (safety standards); economic expectations about product performance are a different category of responsibility.
  2. Risk-spreading limits: While strict liability spreads the cost of personal injury (“an overwhelming misfortune”), this rationale does not extend to business losses: “Th[e] rationale in no way justifies requiring the consuming public to pay more for their products so that a manufacturer can insure against the possibility that some of his products will not meet the business needs of some of his customers.” (63 Cal.2d at 18)
  3. UCC distinction: The UCC itself recognizes this distinction—limitation of damages is prima facie unconscionable in personal injury cases but not in commercial loss cases (citing Cal. Com. Code 2719).

Gray Area — Dicta vs. Holding

Justice Peters concurred/dissented, arguing that everything about the tort/economic-loss distinction was dicta because the case was decided on express warranty grounds alone: “The majority, having found in favor of the plaintiff on the theory of an express warranty, completely decided the case. There was no need to discuss the strict liability doctrine. Everything said by the majority on that subject is obviously dicta.” (63 Cal.2d at 20). However, virtually every subsequent court has treated the majority discussion as the foundational holding creating the ELD.

B. East River Steamship Corp. v. Transamerica Delaval Inc. — Supreme Court Adoption

East River Steamship Corp. v. Transamerica Delaval Inc., 476 U.S. 858, 106 S.Ct. 2295, 90 L.Ed.2d 865 (1986)

Defendant designed and manufactured turbine propulsion systems for four supertankers. The turbines failed due to design and manufacturing flaws. Only the turbine components themselves were damaged—no other part of the ship was damaged and no one was injured. Plaintiffs sued in negligence and strict products liability.

Settled Law — Holding

Unanimous Court (Blackmun, J.) adopted Seely’s approach for federal maritime law:

“[W]e adopt an approach similar to Seely and hold that a manufacturer in a commercial relationship has no duty under either a negligence or strict products-liability theory to prevent a product from injuring itself.” 476 U.S. at 871
“When a product injures only itself the reasons for imposing a tort duty are weak and those for leaving the party to its contractual remedies are strong.” 476 U.S. at 871

The test is binary, turning on the nature of the injury, not the manner of failure:

  • Tort recovery AVAILABLE: Product causes physical injury to a person or damage to “other property.”
  • Tort recovery BARRED: Product injures only itself, resulting in purely economic loss.
“Even when the harm to the product itself occurs through an abrupt, accident-like event, the resulting loss due to repair costs, decreased value, and lost profits is essentially the failure of the purchaser to receive the benefit of its bargain.” 476 U.S. at 871

The Court expressly rejected intermediate tests turning on degree of risk or manner of injury: “We find the intermediate and minority land-based positions unsatisfactory. The intermediate positions, which essentially turn on the degree of risk, are too indeterminate to enable manufacturers easily to structure their business behavior.” (476 U.S. at 870)

Integrated system theory: Component parts (steam reversing rings) damaging the whole turbine did not constitute damage to “other property”: “Since all but the very simplest of machines have component parts, [a contrary] holding would require a finding of ‘property damage’ in virtually every case where a product damages itself. Such a holding would eliminate the distinction between warranty and strict products liability.” (476 U.S. at 867)

Policy rationale:

  1. Commercial parties can allocate risk through contract.
  2. Warranty liability has built-in limitations; tort liability is indefinite.
  3. No large disparities in bargaining power in commercial settings.
  4. Warning: “It is clear, however, that if this development were allowed to progress too far, contract law would drown in a sea of tort.” (476 U.S. at 866, citing Gilmore, The Death of Contract)

Gray Area — Services vs. Products

East River did not address whether services are “products” for ELD purposes. The only mention is passing dicta: “or, as in this case, experiences increased costs in performing a service” (476 U.S. at 871). This silence is significant for software cases, where the product/service distinction is central.

II. Software-Specific Applications of the Economic Loss Doctrine

A. Cases Where ELD WAS Applied to Software

1. Rockport Pharmacy, Inc. v. Digital Simplistics, Inc.

53 F.3d 195 (8th Cir. 1995)

Custom computer system (hardware + software) for pharmacy operations failed to perform. The Eighth Circuit applied ELD under Missouri law, barring tort claims for purely economic loss. Critically, the court rejected the argument that software was a “service” rather than a “product”. The nature of the claim (disappointed expectations in product performance) determined ELD applicability, not the tangibility of software.

2. RRX Industries, Inc. v. Lab-Con, Inc.

772 F.2d 543 (9th Cir. 1985)

Defective software in a labeling machine caused economic loss. The Ninth Circuit applied ELD to bar tort recovery for economic loss from the software defect.

3. Travelers Indemnity Co. v. Heller Financial, Inc.

2002 WL 31896557 (N.D. Ill. 2002)

Applied ELD to bar tort claims in a software licensing dispute where only economic damages resulted.

Settled Law

When software defects cause purely economic loss (no physical injury to person or other property), the majority of courts that have addressed the issue apply the economic loss doctrine to bar tort claims, regardless of whether software is characterized as a “product” or “service.” The determining factor is the nature of the damages, not the characterization of software.

B. Cases Where Courts REFUSED to Apply ELD to Software

1. Spear Mfg., Inc. v. Liberty Insurance Co.

31 F.Supp.2d 734 (E.D. Mich. 1999)

Under Michigan law, the court found software was not a “product” for strict liability purposes and declined to apply the ELD framework because software fell outside products liability entirely. This is a narrow refusal—the court did not hold that tort claims were available, only that the ELD (a products liability doctrine) did not apply.

2. Hughes v. Lord

No. 93-2036, 1994 WL 713794 (E.D. Pa. Dec. 21, 1994)

Court declined to treat software as a “good” under the UCC, leaving tort claims potentially available rather than dismissing under ELD.

Gray Area — The Threshold Question

The unresolved threshold question is: Is the ELD even the right framework for software? If software is not a “product” for products liability purposes (as some courts hold), then the ELD—a doctrine rooted in products liability—may not apply by its own terms. But most courts reach the ELD analysis without first resolving the product/service question, focusing instead on the nature of damages.

III. Software as Product vs. Service vs. Mixed Transaction

Settled Law

  • Software embedded in hardware: Courts overwhelmingly treat the entire system as a “product” subject to ELD (Rockport Pharmacy).
  • Mass-market/shrink-wrap software: ELD more likely to apply because the transaction resembles a sale of goods; UCC Article 2 governs; EULAs typically disclaim tort liability.
  • Custom software: More likely to be characterized as a service contract, potentially placing the dispute outside ELD scope entirely (though some courts still apply ELD by analogy).

Jurisdictional Variations

  • Michigan: Software is not a “product” for strict liability (Spear Mfg.). ELD may not apply.
  • Missouri: ELD applies to software/hardware systems regardless of software tangibility (Rockport Pharmacy).
  • Pennsylvania: ELD does not apply to contracts for services at all.
  • Tennessee: Tennessee Supreme Court declined to extend ELD beyond products liability in 2023, holding the doctrine applies only in the products liability context.

IV. The “Sudden and Accidental” Exception

No reported case applies the “sudden and calamitous” exception specifically to software.

The “sudden and accidental” exception (from the Illinois intermediate rule, as in Trans States Airlines v. Pratt & Whitney) requires a dangerous defect causing sudden physical damage to other property. This factual pattern is extraordinarily difficult to establish with software defects, which typically cause gradual or systemic economic loss rather than sudden physical destruction. The exception is theoretically available but practically inapplicable to software.

V. Mass-Market vs. Custom Software

Gray Area

No court has explicitly held that the ELD applies differently to mass-market vs. custom software. However, the analytical pathway differs:

  • Mass-market: UCC Article 2 applies (sale of goods); ELD follows naturally as a products liability doctrine.
  • Custom software: May be characterized as a service contract under common law; if so, ELD (a products liability doctrine) may not apply by its own terms. But courts in some jurisdictions apply ELD by analogy to all commercial transactions.

Minority View / Scholarly Critique

Legal scholars argue that applying ELD to software is fundamentally misguided because: (1) software is not a tangible “product” that can “injure itself” in the physical sense East River envisioned; (2) EULAs are contracts of adhesion, so the “parties allocated risk through contract” rationale fails; (3) the information asymmetry between developer and user is far greater than in traditional commercial transactions. See NYU Law Review, “Software Torts and Software Contracts: Reframing the Developer’s Duty,” 100 N.Y.U. L. Rev. (2015).

Subtopic 2: Tort Theories for Software Errors

I. Negligent Misrepresentation — Restatement (Second) of Torts §552

A. Elements of §552

Settled Law — Actual Legal Standard

As applied in Bily v. Arthur Young (infra), the elements of §552(2) are:

  1. The defendant, in the course of their business, profession, employment, or any other transaction in which they have a pecuniary interest, supplies false information for the guidance of others in their business transactions;
  2. The defendant failed to exercise reasonable care or competence in obtaining or communicating the information;
  3. The plaintiff suffered pecuniary loss caused by justifiable reliance upon the information;
  4. Scope limitation (§552(2)(a)): The loss was suffered by a person or one of a limited group of persons for whose benefit and guidance the defendant intended to supply the information or knew that the recipient intended to supply it; AND
  5. Transaction limitation (§552(2)(b)): The loss was suffered through reliance upon the information in a transaction that the defendant intended the information to influence or knew that the recipient so intended, or in a substantially similar transaction.

Elements (4) and (5) constitute the “near-privity” limitations. Foreseeability alone is insufficient to establish these elements.

Structural distinction critical to software: Restatement §551 (failure to convey information) vs. §552 (affirmative false information). A manufacturer’s failure to disclose product defects (§551-type conduct) does not trigger §552 liability. A software developer’s failure to disclose bugs would not qualify as §552 negligent misrepresentation.

B. Bily v. Arthur Young & Co. — Leading Case on §552 Limitations

Bily v. Arthur Young & Co., 3 Cal.4th 370, 834 P.2d 745, 16 Cal.Rptr.2d 804 (1992)

Settled Law — Holding

The California Supreme Court adopted Restatement §552 for negligent misrepresentation by accountants but imposed strict limitations:

  • Rejected foreseeability as the basis for liability: “[T]he fact that a professional’s negligence may cause harm to an indefinite class of persons does not of itself justify imposing liability for that harm.”
  • Requires “intent to benefit” a specific limited group—foreseeability alone does not satisfy the §552(2)(a) scope limitation.
  • The ELD bars general negligence claims for economic loss; §552 provides a narrow, privity-based exception.

Minority View — Dissent

Justices Kennard and Mosk dissented, arguing the majority “revived an anachronistic privity barrier” that protected wealthy investors at the expense of those who reasonably relied on professional information. They argued foreseeability should be sufficient.

C. Has §552 Been Applied to Software Errors?

No reported decision has applied Restatement §552 to recover for software defects or errors.

Publications International, Ltd. v. Mindtree Limited (N.D. Ill. 2014): The court explicitly rejected §552 applicability to software, holding that a software development firm is NOT an “information provider” under the negligent misrepresentation exception to the economic loss doctrine because the defendant was hired to provide a product (software) and technical services—not information—with any information provided being “purely tangential or secondary” to the main purpose of the contract.

Settled Law

Software developers are categorically outside the class of defendants to whom §552 applies. The Restatement defines §552 as applying to defendants “in the business of supplying information for the guidance of others”—accountants, architects, title companies, surveyors. Software developers create tools/products, not informational reports. The Publications Int’l v. Mindtree holding establishes this as a structural mismatch: §552 is designed for informational reports, not software tools.

II. The Negligent Misrepresentation Exception to the Economic Loss Doctrine

A. Bilt-Rite Contractors v. The Architectural Studio (Pennsylvania)

Bilt-Rite Contractors, Inc. v. The Architectural Studio, 581 Pa. 545, 866 A.2d 312 (2005)

Settled Law — Holding

“Thus, we hold that the economic loss rule does not apply to claims of negligent misrepresentation sounding under Section 552.” 581 Pa. at 556, 866 A.2d at 318

Pennsylvania adopted a categorical exception—no additional limitations beyond §552 own text. Unlike California Bily, Pennsylvania did not add foreseeability limitations. The court reasoned it would be “nonsensical” to allow a §552 claim to proceed only to find ELD bars recovery, particularly where the plaintiff has no contractual relationship with the defendant.

B. Moorman Manufacturing Co. v. National Tank Co. (Illinois)

Moorman Manufacturing Co. v. National Tank Co., 91 Ill.2d 69, 435 N.E.2d 443 (1982)

Settled Law — Three Moorman Exceptions

Illinois ELD bars tort recovery for purely economic loss from product defects. Three exceptions subsequently developed:

  1. Sudden and dangerous occurrence: Damage (personal injury or property damage) resulting from a sudden, dangerous, or calamitous occurrence.
  2. Intentional false misrepresentation (fraud): Elements: (a) false statement of material fact; (b) knowledge or belief that the statement is false; (c) intent to induce action; (d) justifiable reliance; (e) resulting damage.
  3. Negligent misrepresentation by information suppliers: Under Restatement §552, where the defendant is in the business of supplying information for the guidance of others in their business transactions.

The original Moorman opinion (1982) only explicitly addressed innocent misrepresentation, barring it because it would create de facto strict liability. The three exceptions were developed in subsequent Illinois case law.

Even in jurisdictions that recognize the §552 exception to ELD, the threshold question remains: Is a software developer an “information supplier” under §552? Publications Int’l v. Mindtree says no. No court has said yes. The exception exists in theory but may be structurally unavailable for software claims.

III. Fraud Claims and EULA Disclaimers

A. The General Rule: Fraud Survives ELD

Settled Law

Fraud (intentional misrepresentation) is recognized as an exception to the economic loss doctrine in virtually every jurisdiction that has adopted the ELD. The reasoning is that a party who intentionally defrauds another cannot hide behind the ELD, which is designed to preserve the contract-tort boundary—not to shield intentional wrongdoing.

B. The Critical Distinction: Pre-Formation vs. Post-Formation Fraud

Budgetel Inns, Inc. v. Micros Systems, Inc.

8 F.Supp.2d 1137 (E.D. Wis. 1998)

This case established an important nuance for software: fraud in the inducement (pre-formation fraud based on an independent duty of honesty) survived ELD at the motion-to-dismiss stage. However, fraud occurring after contract execution would be barred by ELD. The distinction is between fraud that induced the contract (independent duty) and fraud about the quality of the product delivered (contractual duty subsumed by ELD).

Settled Law — Fraud vs. EULA Disclaimers

No case was found where a fraud claim survived a specific, targeted disclaimer in a software agreement. The key cases establish:

1. Int’l Bus. Mach. Corp. v. Lufkin Indus., LLC

No. 17-0666 (Tex. Sup. Ct. Mar. 15, 2019)

Highest authority on this issue. SAP software implementation case. The Texas Supreme Court held that specific anti-reliance clauses enumerating disclaimed representations (completion dates, hours, charges, results, customer experiences) barred fraudulent inducement and string-along fraud claims. The court expressly distinguished generic merger clauses as insufficient but found specific anti-reliance clauses meet the standard.

2. Diedrich v. Wolters Kluwer

2024 WL 291156 (S.D.N.Y. Jan. 25, 2024)

Tax software case. The plaintiff brought only breach of contract—no fraud claims were pleaded. The court enforced two key disclaimers: (1) “THE ENTIRE RISK AS TO THE QUALITY AND PERFORMANCE OF THE PRODUCT(S), INCLUDING ELECTRONIC FILING” and (2) specific disclaimer that use “will not satisfy any statutory or regulatory obligations.” This case provides no authority on whether fraud survives EULA disclaimers because no fraud claim was at issue.

3. SensorTower, Inc. v. Similarweb Ltd.

2025 NY Slip Op. 32998(U) (N.Y. Sup. Ct., Commercial Div. July 24, 2025)

Specific disclaimer of data quality/adequacy coupled with “as is” and entire agreement clause barred fraudulent inducement counterclaim about data quality. Trial court, no reporter citation.

Actual Legal Standard

Generic merger clauses alone will NOT bar fraud. But specific anti-reliance clauses that enumerate the precise representations being disclaimed WILL bar fraud—even fraudulent inducement. The test from IBM v. Lufkin: does the disclaimer specifically address the subject matter of the alleged fraudulent representation?

IV. Negligence Per Se for Software Errors

No reported case applies negligence per se to software errors.

No case was found where violation of IRS regulations, tax code provisions, or any other statute created negligence per se liability for software calculation errors. The theoretical pathway exists (violation of statute establishes duty and breach), but multiple barriers prevent it in practice:

  1. Causal chain: Negligence per se requires that the statute was designed to prevent the type of harm suffered. IRS regulations govern tax preparers and taxpayers, not software developers.
  2. ELD as earlier disposition: Courts dismiss under ELD before reaching the negligence per se analysis.
  3. EULA arbitration clauses: Most software agreements mandate arbitration, keeping these claims out of courts entirely.
  4. No duty owed: The fundamental tort hurdle—establishing that a software developer owes a duty of care to end users for calculation accuracy—remains unresolved and unfavorable.

V. Consumer Protection Statutes (UDAP) and Software Calculation Errors

No reported case applies state UDAP/consumer fraud statutes to software calculation errors.

Existing UDAP/enforcement actions against tax software companies involve deceptive advertising only, never calculation defects:

  • Intuit (TurboTax): $141M FTC settlement for deceptive “free” advertising practices.
  • H&R Block: $7M FTC settlement for deceptive advertising.
  • H&R Block: $1.6M Los Angeles City Attorney settlement for deceptive advertising.

None of these involved claims that the software produced incorrect tax calculations. The UDAP pathway for calculation errors remains entirely unlitigated.

Subtopic 3: Privity Requirements and Third-Party Liability

I. Can Third Parties (IRS, State Tax Agencies) Sue the Software Developer Directly?

Settled Law

No. Government agencies (IRS, state tax authorities) have no tort or contract claim against a software developer for errors in tax preparation software. The government recourse is against the taxpayer who filed an incorrect return, not against the software tool the taxpayer used. No reported case was found where any government agency sued a software developer for calculation errors.

II. UCC §2-318: Third Party Beneficiaries of Warranties

A. The Three Alternatives

Actual Legal Standard

B (Intermediate)Any natural person; must be reasonable to expect usePersonal injury onlyNo (non-waivable for personal injury)
C (Broadest)Any person; must be reasonable to expect usePersonal injury or property damageNon-waivable for personal injury; waivable for property damage

Settled Law — Critical Structural Limitation

None of the three alternatives creates a non-waivable right for pure economic loss—the exclusive damage type in software defect cases. Alternative C extends to property damage, but software calculation errors do not cause property damage. UCC §2-318 is effectively inapplicable to software warranty privity for economic loss claims.

No case was found applying UCC §2-318 to software.

Jurisdictional Variations

State adoption of UCC §2-318 alternatives is incomplete and inconsistent. Only Mississippi was confirmed as adopting Alternative A in the research. Complete state-by-state adoption data requires paywalled database access. However, this is largely academic for software because none of the alternatives reach pure economic loss.

III. Can a User Without Direct Purchase Sue the Developer?

No case was found dismissing a software warranty claim solely on privity grounds where the plaintiff was an indirect purchaser (e.g., employee using employer license). Courts resolve these cases via ELD and disclaimer analysis before reaching privity, making privity a moot issue in practice.

A. Advent Systems Ltd. v. Unisys Corp.

Advent Systems Ltd. v. Unisys Corp., 925 F.2d 670 (3d Cir. 1991)

Advent developed software in the UK; Unisys distributed it in the US. End-users sued Advent for breach of warranty. The Third Circuit held that UCC warranties could extend to remote purchasers under certain circumstances, but the case is primarily cited for its holding that software is a “good” under UCC Article 2. The privity analysis was fact-specific and did not establish a general rule for third-party software users.

IV. Third-Party Beneficiary Theory for Software Licenses

Settled Law — General Standard

A non-party may sue for breach of contract only if it is an intended (not incidental) beneficiary, and “even then, even if not mentioned as a party to the contract, the parties intent to benefit the third party must be apparent from the face of the contract.” (New York standard, applied broadly). Courts are reluctant to find third-party beneficiary status absent clear contractual language.

Gray Area — Software License Context

No case was found where a software license user successfully established third-party beneficiary status to sue the developer directly. The typical EULA structure—licensing the software to the purchaser/entity, not to individual end-users—makes it extremely difficult for an individual employee to argue they are an intended beneficiary. The SFC v. Vizio case (discussed below regarding GPL) represents the only recent significant third-party beneficiary case in software, but it involves open-source licenses, not commercial EULAs.

SFC v. Vizio — Open Source Third-Party Beneficiary (Distinguishing Case)

SFC v. Vizio, No. 21-cv-00299 (C.D. Cal. 2024)

The Software Freedom Conservancy survived summary judgment on the issue of whether it is a third-party beneficiary under the GPLv2 and LGPLv2.1 licenses. If SFC ultimately prevails, any end user could bring claims under open-source license terms. However, this case is fundamentally distinguishable from commercial EULA contexts: open-source licenses are explicitly designed to benefit the public, whereas commercial EULAs are designed to limit liability.

V. Privity in Software Context — Practical Reality

Settled Law

Privity is rarely the dispositive issue in software liability cases because other defenses dispose of claims first:

  1. Economic Loss Doctrine: Bars tort claims for economic loss regardless of privity.
  2. EULA Disclaimers: Disclaim all warranties and limit liability; enforceable in most commercial contexts.
  3. Arbitration Clauses: Most EULAs mandate binding arbitration, keeping claims out of courts entirely.
  4. No Duty: Even if privity existed, courts have not recognized a duty of care for software calculation accuracy.

Privity is thus a “backstop” defense that is rarely reached because earlier defenses are dispositive.

Master Summary: What Is Clearly Established vs. What Remains Unclear

Settled Law (High Confidence)

  1. The economic loss doctrine bars tort recovery for purely economic loss from defective products (Seely; East River).
  2. When applied to software, ELD bars tort claims where only economic damages result (Rockport Pharmacy; RRX Industries; Travelers Indemnity).
  3. Fraud is an exception to ELD in virtually every jurisdiction.
  4. Specific anti-reliance clauses in software agreements bar fraud claims, including fraudulent inducement (IBM v. Lufkin).
  5. Software developers are NOT “information suppliers” under Restatement §552 (Publications Int’l v. Mindtree).
  6. No §552 negligent misrepresentation claim has succeeded for software defects.
  7. UCC §2-318 does not extend warranty rights to third parties for pure economic loss.
  8. Government agencies cannot sue software developers for calculation errors.
  9. EULA disclaimers of warranty and liability are enforceable in commercial software contexts (Diedrich v. Wolters Kluwer).

Gray Areas (Disputed or Unclear)

  1. Whether software is a “product” for ELD purposes—courts reach ELD without resolving this threshold question.
  2. Whether ELD applies to custom software (service contract characterization) vs. mass-market software (goods characterization).
  3. Whether the §552 exception to ELD could theoretically apply to software in jurisdictions like Pennsylvania that adopted the categorical Bilt-Rite approach—blocked by the “information supplier” threshold, but no court has directly addressed this two-step analysis for software.
  4. Whether a pre-formation fraud claim could survive both ELD and specific disclaimers in a software context—theoretically possible but no case found.
  5. Whether an employee using employer software license could sue the developer as a third-party beneficiary—no case found.

Minority Views / Scholarly Critiques

  1. ELD should not apply to software because software cannot “injure itself” in the physical sense East River envisioned (scholarly critique).
  2. The “parties allocated risk through contract” rationale fails for EULAs, which are contracts of adhesion (NYU Law Review).
  3. Information asymmetry between developer and user far exceeds that in traditional commercial transactions, undermining ELD policy rationale (scholarly critique).
  4. Tennessee (2023) declined to extend ELD beyond products liability, suggesting a narrowing trend that could benefit software plaintiffs if adopted elsewhere.
  5. Justice Peters dissent in Seely argued the entire ELD foundation is dicta.
  6. Justices Kennard and Mosk dissent in Bily argued for foreseeability-based §552 liability.
