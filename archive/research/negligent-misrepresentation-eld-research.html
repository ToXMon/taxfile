SOURCE 1: Bily v. Arthur Young & Co.

(a) Full Citation

Bily v. Arthur Young & Co., 3 Cal. 4th 370, 11 Cal. Rptr. 2d 51, 834 P.2d 745 (1992).

Court: Supreme Court of California | Opinion filed: August 27, 1992

(b) Precise Holding on Negligent Misrepresentation Under Restatement §552

HOLDING

The California Supreme Court adopted Restatement (Second) of Torts §552 as the governing standard for negligent misrepresentation by accountants. The court held that an auditor is liable for negligent misrepresentation only if the loss was suffered by a person or a limited group of persons for whose benefit and guidance the accountant intended to supply the information (or knew the recipient intended to supply it), and the loss resulted from reliance upon the information in a transaction that the accountant intended to influence (or knew the recipient intended to influence) or a substantially similar transaction.

"An auditor may, however, be held liable for negligent misrepresentations in an audit report to those persons who act in reliance upon those misrepresentations in a transaction which the auditor intended to influence, in accordance with the rule of section 552 of the Restatement Second of Torts, as adopted and discussed below." — vLex summary of majority opinion
"Holding negligent accountants liable to foreseeable users of audit opinions will subject accountants to 'a claim for all sums of money ever loaned to or invested in the client' and result in 'vast numbers of suits and limitless financial exposure.'" — Majority opinion, ante, p. 400

(c) LIMITATIONS on §552 Liability: Foreseeability & Near-Privity

Foreseeability Limitation

HOLDING

The court explicitly rejected foreseeability of reliance as the basis for §552 liability. A supplier of information bears no legal responsibility if they "merely know of the ever-present possibility of repetition to anyone, and the possibility of action in reliance upon [the information] on the part of anyone to whom it may be repeated." There is no liability to others who become aware of the representation and act upon it, even if the defendant should reasonably have foreseen such a possibility.

Near-Privity / "Intent to Benefit" Limitation

HOLDING

While the court rejected the strict "privity barrier" for negligence claims, it adopted the Restatement's "intent to benefit" standard for misrepresentation, which functions as a limiting principle by excluding liability to a "faceless or unresolved class of persons." The supplier must manifest an intent to supply the information for a specific use involving defined economic parameters.

DICTA

The majority's reasoning about "vast numbers of suits and limitless financial exposure" is arguably dicta supporting the limitation rather than part of the holding itself. The actual holding is the adoption of §552 with its built-in limitations.

(d) Adoption or Rejection of Negligent Misrepresentation Exception to ELD

HOLDING

The court effectively adopted negligent misrepresentation as an exception to the bar on recovering pure economic loss under general negligence, but narrowly. The court distinguished between two torts:

  • General negligence: No duty owed to non-clients for economic loss. ELD applies fully.
  • Negligent misrepresentation (§552): Duty exists but is narrowly circumscribed by the "intent to benefit" requirement. ELD does NOT bar recovery if §552 elements are met.

Thus, California recognizes negligent misrepresentation under §552 as a narrow pathway to recover economic losses that would otherwise be barred by the economic loss doctrine in a general negligence framework.

(e) Elements of §552 as Applied

ELEMENTS (§552(2))
  1. The defendant, in the course of their business, profession, employment, or any other transaction in which they have a pecuniary interest, supplies false information for the guidance of others in their business transactions;
  2. The defendant failed to exercise reasonable care or competence in obtaining or communicating the information;
  3. The plaintiff suffered pecuniary loss caused by their justifiable reliance upon the information;
  4. Scope limitation: The loss was suffered by a person or one of a limited group of persons for whose benefit and guidance the defendant intended to supply the information or knew that the recipient intended to supply it; AND
  5. Transaction limitation: The loss was suffered through reliance upon the information in a transaction that the defendant intended the information to influence or knew that the recipient so intended, or in a substantially similar transaction.
Critical Note: Elements (4) and (5) are the "near-privity" limitations that Bily emphasized. Foreseeability alone is insufficient to establish these elements.

(f) Dissenting/Concurring Opinions

DISSENT — Justice Kennard, joined by Justice Mosk

Justice Kennard argued that the majority inappropriately revived the "anachronistic privity barrier" for negligence liability and adopted an arbitrary rule for negligent misrepresentation that granted negligent accountants "broad immunity."

  • Foreseeability argument: Justice Kennard argued for a foreseeability standard, asserting that the distinction between negligence and negligent misrepresentation was "anomalous" because the same standard of care applies to the same conduct.
  • Policy critique: The dissent contended that the majority's rule protected wealthy and financially savvy investors who could afford private audits, while punishing small investors and lenders who relied on independent audit reports.

No separate concurring opinion. Majority authored by Chief Justice Lucas, joined by Justices Panelli, Arabian, Baxter, and George.

SOURCE 2: Bilt-Rite Contractors v. The Architectural Studio

(a) Full Citation

Bilt-Rite Contractors, Inc. v. The Architectural Studio, 581 Pa. 545, 866 A.2d 312 (Pa. 2005).

Court: Supreme Court of Pennsylvania | Decided: January 19, 2005 | Docket No. 74 MAP 2002

Note: Atlantic Reporter citation (866 A.2d 312) reconstructed from standard Pennsylvania Supreme Court citation format; confirmed by multiple secondary sources referencing the case at 581 Pa. 545.

(b) Precise Holding: Negligent Misrepresentation as Exception to ELD

HOLDING

"Thus, we hold that the economic loss rule does not apply to claims of negligent misrepresentation sounding under Section 552."

"Applying the economic loss doctrine in such a context would be 'nonsensical' because it would allow a party to pursue an action only to find that, once the elements of the cause of action are shown, the party is unable to recover for its losses, particularly where the plaintiff has no contractual relationship with the defendant and therefore no contractual recourse." — Majority opinion

The court adopted Restatement (Second) of Torts §552 as the basis for negligent misrepresentation claims and explicitly held that the economic loss doctrine does not bar such claims when they "sound under Section 552."

(c) Test for When Negligent Misrepresentation Escapes ELD

HOLDING

Negligent misrepresentation escapes the economic loss doctrine when the claim "sounds under Section 552," which requires:

TEST FOR §552 EXCEPTION TO ELD (PA)
  1. The defendant is in the business of supplying information for the guidance of others in their business transactions (e.g., architects, attorneys, surveyors, banks, abstractors of title, inspectors of goods, operators of ticker services);
  2. The defendant supplies false information in the course of that business, profession, employment, or other transaction in which they have a pecuniary interest;
  3. The plaintiff suffers pecuniary loss caused by justifiable reliance upon the information;
  4. The defendant failed to exercise reasonable care or competence in obtaining or communicating the information;
  5. Scope limitation: Liability is limited to loss suffered by a person or limited group for whose benefit and guidance the defendant intended to supply the information or knew the recipient intended to supply it;
  6. Transaction limitation: Loss must occur through reliance in a transaction the defendant intended to influence or knew the recipient so intended, or a substantially similar transaction.
Key Distinction from Bily: Unlike California's Bily, Pennsylvania's Bilt-Rite did not add additional "foreseeability" limitations beyond those already in §552. The Pennsylvania court adopted §552's framework as a complete exception to ELD without further narrowing.

(d) Application to Software vs. Design/Professional Services

NO SOFTWARE APPLICATION

The opinion does not mention software. The holding is specifically scoped to those "in the business of supplying information," with the court citing historical examples: "attorneys, abstractors of title, surveyors, inspectors of goods, operators of ticker services, and banks." The holding was explicitly applied to the "architect/contractor scenario" involving architects and design professionals supplying plans and specifications for use by bidders.

Implication for software: Whether a software developer qualifies as being "in the business of supplying information" under Bilt-Rite is an open question in Pennsylvania. The court's enumerated list of information suppliers does not include software developers, and the factual context involved physical design documents, not digital products. However, the court's reasoning is not expressly limited to physical documents — the key question is whether the software constitutes "information" rather than a "product."

SOURCE 3: Moorman Manufacturing Co. v. National Tank Co.

(a) Full Citation

Moorman Manufacturing Co. v. National Tank Co., 91 Ill. 2d 69, 435 N.E.2d 443 (1982).

Court: Supreme Court of Illinois | Opinion filed: February 19, 1982

(b) Precise Holding Establishing Illinois Economic Loss Doctrine

HOLDING

"[The plaintiff] cannot recover for solely economic loss under the tort theories of strict liability, negligence and innocent misrepresentation." Purely economic losses resulting from a product defect are not recoverable in tort and must be pursued under contract law — specifically, the warranty provisions of the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC).

"The cost of repair and loss of profits or income were economic losses which could not be recovered under the tort theories named in the complaint." — Majority opinion
"Economic losses are not recoverable under strict liability in tort." — Majority opinion

(c) The "Failure to Convey Information" vs. "Affirmative Misrepresentation" Distinction

HOLDING (Original Moorman)

The Moorman court addressed innocent misrepresentation specifically and held it barred by the economic loss doctrine. The court reasoned that imposing liability for innocent misrepresentation resulting in economic loss would effectively create a form of strict liability, which is not supported by tort law for purely economic losses.

"A plaintiff cannot recover in tort for purely economic losses based on innocent misrepresentation; the remedy for such losses lies under the warranty provisions of the UCC." — Majority opinion

The §551/§552 Structural Distinction (Critical Context):

The "failure to convey information" vs. "affirmative misrepresentation" distinction is inherent in the Restatement (Second) of Torts structure:

  • Restatement §551 covers failure to convey information (negligent nondisclosure) — a duty to disclose arises only in specific relationships or circumstances.
  • Restatement §552 covers affirmative false information negligently supplied — liability arises when false information is affirmatively provided without reasonable care.

The Moorman court implicitly relied on this distinction when barring innocent misrepresentation: mere failure to convey accurate information (§551-type conduct) in a product context does not escape ELD, because imposing liability would create de facto strict liability. The court left open whether intentional affirmative misrepresentation (fraud/deceit) could escape ELD — and subsequent Illinois cases confirmed it does.

(d) Exceptions to Moorman (As Subsequently Developed)

IMPORTANT TEMPORAL NOTE: The original Moorman opinion (1982) only explicitly addressed innocent misrepresentation. The three exceptions below were developed in subsequent Illinois case law and are now recognized as part of the "Moorman doctrine" framework:
THREE EXCEPTIONS TO MOORMAN/ELD (ILLINOIS)
  1. Sudden and Dangerous Occurrence: When a plaintiff sustains damage (personal injury or property damage) resulting from a sudden, dangerous, or calamitous occurrence.
  2. Intentional False Misrepresentation (Fraud): When a plaintiff's damages are proximately caused by a defendant's intentional, false misrepresentation. Elements: (a) false statement of material fact; (b) knowledge or belief that the statement is false; (c) intent to induce action; (d) justifiable reliance; (e) resulting damage.
  3. Negligent Misrepresentation by Information Suppliers: When a plaintiff's damages are proximately caused by a negligent misrepresentation made by a defendant who is in the business of supplying information for the guidance of others in their business transactions (Restatement §552). This exception was added post-Moorman and is consistent with §552's framework.
DICTA vs. HOLDING

Original Moorman (1982): The bar on innocent misrepresentation was a holding. The court's reasoning about UCC evisceration — "adopting strict liability in tort for economic loss would effectively 'eviscerate' section 2-316 of the UCC" — supports the holding. The court's comments about warranty rules preventing "liability for damages of unknown and unlimited scope" are dicta elaborating policy rationale.

Subsequent exceptions (2) and (3): The intentional misrepresentation and negligent misrepresentation exceptions were developed in later Illinois cases, not in Moorman itself. These are now settled Illinois law but were not part of the original Moorman holding.

Why Moorman Barred Innocent Misrepresentation But Later Allowed Negligent Misrepresentation

The apparent inconsistency is resolved by the §551/§552 distinction and the "in the business of supplying information" requirement:

  • Innocent misrepresentation in Moorman involved a product manufacturer (tank company) whose alleged misrepresentations were incidental to the product sale — not someone in the business of supplying information.
  • Negligent misrepresentation exception (later) applies only to defendants who are in the business of supplying information — a fundamentally different category of defendant (e.g., accountants, architects, title companies).

§552 Applied to Software Errors / Defective Software

Research Results Summary

Exhaustive searches for cases applying Restatement §552 specifically to software errors or defective software yielded no cases where §552 was successfully applied to software defects. The cases found all involve courts rejecting the application of §552 to software or analyzing software misrepresentation under different theories.

CASE 1: Publications International, Ltd. v. Mindtree Limited

Citation: Publications International, Ltd. v. Mindtree Limited, 2014 WL 3687316 (N.D. Ill. 2014)

Court: United States District Court for the Northern District of Illinois

HOLDING

The court held that a software development firm is NOT an "information provider" under the negligent misrepresentation exception to the economic loss doctrine. The defendant was hired to provide a product (software) and technical services — not information. Any information provided was "purely tangential or secondary" to the main purpose of the contract.

"The defendant was hired to provide a product (software) and services (tech assistance) — not information (this in spite of the ironic 'information-technology' title)." — Court analysis

Significance: This is the most directly on-point case for the software/§552 question. The court drew a sharp line between software-as-product and information-as-service, holding that the §552 negligent misrepresentation exception does not apply to software development contracts even when the software involves processing or conveying information.

CASE 2: Budgetel Inns, Inc. v. Micros Systems, Inc.

Citation: Budgetel Inns, Inc. v. Micros Systems, Inc., 8 F. Supp. 2d 1137 (E.D. Wis. 1998)

Court: United States District Court for the Eastern District of Wisconsin

HOLDING

The court initially denied a motion to dismiss plaintiff's fraud in the inducement claim regarding property management system software, finding that fraud claims are "as a rule" not barred by Wisconsin's economic loss doctrine. However, in a subsequent ruling (Budgetel II), the court reconsidered and ultimately ruled that the economic loss doctrine did bar the fraud claim where the misrepresentation related only to the quality of goods promised in the contract.

Facts: Budgetel contracted with Micros for property management system software, system support, and maintenance ($250,000 license fee + ~$150,000/year maintenance). Software allegedly failed to perform as represented.

Significance: Budgetel involved fraud in the inducement (intentional misrepresentation), not negligent misrepresentation under §552. The case illustrates that even the more broadly accepted intentional misrepresentation exception to ELD can be barred when the misrepresentation relates to software quality within a contractual relationship. This makes the §552 negligent misrepresentation pathway for software even more unlikely to succeed.

Key Conclusions

1. The §552/ELD Exception Is Jurisdictionally Fragmented

All three jurisdictions recognize some form of negligent misrepresentation exception to the economic loss doctrine, but the scope varies dramatically. California (Bily) is the most restrictive, requiring a showing of specific intent to benefit that goes beyond §552's text. Pennsylvania (Bilt-Rite) is the most permissive, adopting §552 as a categorical exception without additional limitations. Illinois occupies a middle ground, having developed the exception through post-Moorman case law.

2. The "Information Supplier" Threshold Is the Critical Gatekeeper

Across all three jurisdictions, the fundamental requirement is that the defendant be "in the business of supplying information for the guidance of others in their business transactions." This requirement excludes product manufacturers whose misrepresentations are incidental to product sales (Moorman) and, critically, software developers whose primary product is code, not information (Mindtree).

3. §552 Is Structurally Mismatched to Software Defects

The Restatement §552 framework was designed for informational reports (audits, surveys, title abstracts, design specifications) where: (a) the defendant affirmatively supplies information, (b) the information is intended to guide a specific transaction, and (c) the plaintiff relies on the informational content. Software defects involve a different paradigm: the defendant supplies a tool that processes data, and the defect is in the tool's operation, not in affirmatively supplied informational content. No court has bridged this gap.

4. The Affirmative vs. Non-Disclosure Distinction Remains Crucial

The Restatement's structural distinction between §551 (failure to convey information) and §552 (affirmative false information) operates as a substantive limitation. Moorman's rejection of innocent misrepresentation implicitly relied on this distinction: a manufacturer's failure to disclose product defects (§551-type conduct) does not trigger §552 liability. For software, this means that a developer's failure to disclose bugs would not qualify as §552 negligent misrepresentation.

5. Dicta vs. Holding Distinction Matters for Precedential Value

  • Bily: The actual holding is the adoption of §552 with its limitations. The policy rhetoric about "limitless financial exposure" is dicta.
  • Bilt-Rite: The holding that ELD does not apply to §552 claims is clean and categorical. Discussion of specific professional categories is arguably dicta.
  • Moorman: The holding bars innocent misrepresentation in tort for economic loss. The three exceptions (including §552 negligent misrepresentation) were developed subsequently and are not part of the original holding.
